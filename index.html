Australia’s Conservation Story

Australia protects a very large land area. The maps show where protection sits; the charts ask what kind of protection it is, how it has changed.

Central narrative

The story moves from landscape to biodiversity. First, the dashboard shows Australia’s bioregions and protected-area geography. Then it compares how protection has expanded, which land-use categories dominate, and how threatened animal data we take a look at how animal populations are doing in terrestrial Australia.

  1. Where is conservation land? The map tabs compare bioregions, protected-area locations, and protection percentages.
  2. What kind of protection dominates? The treemap and time-series plots show the structure and growth of the protected estate.
  3. What about animals? The threatened-species plots show both risk composition and monitoring concentration.

About

  • IBRA (Interim Biogeographic Regionalisation for Australia): A scientific framework that divides Australia into distinct ecological regions based on geology, landforms, climate, vegetation, and biodiversity to support conservation planning and environmental management.
  • CAPAD (Collaborative Australian Protected Areas Database): Australia's national database of protected areas, compiling information on the location, size, type, and management of national parks, nature reserves, and other conservation lands across the country.
  • How to use this dashboard: Use the buttons in the main map panel to switch between the four spatial views. All other plots remain visible below as supporting evidence. State colours correspond to the official colours used by Australian jurisdictions.

8. IUCN protected-area categories

The treemap shows what kind of conservation land dominates: strict reserves, national parks, protected landscapes, or sustainable-use areas. The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) is a global environmental organization that develops international standards for nature conservation, including the widely used system for classifying protected areas according to their management objectives and level of protection.

Source: CAPAD 2024 IUCN category area totals.

9. Threat status by animal group

This 100% stacked bar chart compares birds, mammals, and reptiles by showing the share of monitored species in each EPBC threat category. TSX tracks changes in threatened species populations over time, while the EPBC Act provides Australia’s national legal framework for protecting threatened species and ecological communities.

Source: Australian Threatened Species Index 2025 terrestrial animal dataset, aggregated by unique species and EPBC status.

10. Most monitored threatened animals

This chart shows the threatened animal species with the highest number of monitoring records in the Threatened Species Index (TSX). Species with more records have generally received greater conservation monitoring effort, often reflecting their ecological importance, threat level, or recovery priorities.

TSX (Threatened Species Index): The Threatened Species Index is an Australian biodiversity indicator that tracks changes in the population abundance of threatened species over time, showing how species at risk of extinction are faring.

EPBC Act (Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act): The Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 is Australia's primary national environmental law, providing legal protection for threatened species, ecological communities, and other matters of national environmental significance.

Source: Australian Threatened Species Index 2025 terrestrial animal monitoring records.
